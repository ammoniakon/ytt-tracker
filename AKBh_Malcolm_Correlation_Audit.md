# AKBh × Malcolm Transcripts — Correlation Audit

**Purpose:** Audit Module C (revised) against Malcolm Smith's two-series Abhidharma transcripts to determine which Sangpo volumes are actually covered in Malcolm's lectures and where Module C's reading assignments outstrip Malcolm's coverage. This index supports course design: any AKBh week not directly grounded in a Malcolm transcript is a place where you'll be reading the volume without the transcript scaffold and may want to either (a) supplement with secondary scholarship, (b) defer that week's depth to a later semester, or (c) lean on the Madhyamaka co-week for analytical traction.

## Part II — Malcolm Smith's Two-Series Coverage Map

### Malcolm's Own Framing (from the transcripts)

From **Series I Class 1**, Malcolm describes his eight-week course design: *"There's four chapters \[in this series\]. There's two in the first volume, that's correct, and there's two in the second. That's why there's four volumes for eight chapters, plus a ninth chapter, which is called the refutation of the self."* He then specifies: *"There's eight weeks, and there's four chapters... figure out how many verses there are in each chapter, divide that number by two, and read half the verses for each chapter prior to each class' meeting."*

From **Series II Class 1**, Malcolm resumes: *"We've successfully completed chapters 1, 2, and 3 of the Abhidharmakosha Vasha. So, this next six weeks... topics that we will cover will be the second half of the second topic of the Four Noble Truths, the cause of suffering, which is karma, the karma chapter, and primarily the first chapter of the third topic, which is the cessation of suffering, which is the chapter on afflictions. Time permitting, on the last day, or maybe, depending on how fast we go through the material, the last class, or the last two classes, may consist of miscellaneous topics from the remaining chapters. For example, the 40 Dhyanas, and the more detailed refutation of the self, persons, refutation of the existence of God, and these kinds of things. But that's not the path, since we're primarily Mahayana practitioners, and we don't really practice according to the paths and stages of the Sarvastivadins... there's not a lot of need to spend a great deal of time discussing their paths and stages, which is what the final three chapters consist of."*

### Critical Pedagogical Note from Malcolm Himself

Two transcript moments worth pinning down because they shape what is realistically engageable:

**On the final three chapters (Chs. 6, 7, 8):** *"Since we're primarily Mahayana practitioners, and we don't really practice according to the paths and stages of the Sarvastivadins, there's not a lot of need to spend a great deal of time discussing their paths and stages, which is what the final three chapters consist of. Although, there's interesting things to discuss in the final three chapters. But principally, what's most important is that all the presentations of karma are summarized here, and all the presentations of how the afflictions function..."*

Translation: Malcolm regards Chs. 6, 7, 8 as containing the Sarvāstivādin path-architecture that Mahāyāna practitioners are not following. He is not skipping them out of laziness; he is making a doctrinal-positioning argument that for our lineage context, the Mahāyāna path structure (which the Madhyamaka curriculum, the Dzogchen curriculum, and the Trilogy of Rest engage) supersedes the Sarvāstivādin one. The *content* of Ch. 6 (path of seeing, 16 moments, etc.) and Ch. 7 (jñānas) and Ch. 8 (dhyāna-factor architecture) is interesting but not soteriologically operative for us.

**On Ch. 9 (Pudgalaviniścaya):** From Series II Class 2: *"The true value of the pronoun 'to whom' or 'of whom' will be examined in the chapter on the refutation of the pudgala. That's in the final chapter which I hope we can get to."* This is the conditional language of a teacher hoping to reach a chapter but not having structurally allocated time to it. Whether Class 6 of Series II actually delivers the full Ch. 9 treatment or only an overview is unclear from the transcripts available.

**On the refutation of Īśvara:** Critically, the philosophical work of refuting a creator God (Īśvara, Puruṣa, Pradhāna) **is performed in Series I Class 5** during the causes-and-conditions material (Ch. 2 of the AKBh, Vol 1\) — not in Ch. 9\. Malcolm uses Vasubandhu's argument that *"the world does not proceed from a single cause"* to dispatch the Īśvara position long before reaching Ch. 9\. By the time Ch. 9 itself comes around, the substantive philosophical work has already been done; what remains in Ch. 9 is the detailed extension to the Vātsīputrīya *pudgala* and the more developed scriptural argumentation.

### Not Covered (Implicit Gap): Ch. 7

**Ch. 7 (Jñānanirdeśa — Exposition of the Cognitions)** is the most striking absence: it does not appear as a reading assignment anywhere in the revised Module C, and Malcolm does not cover it systematically. The chapter treats the ten cognitions (*jñānas*) and the qualities of the Buddha — material that intersects with the Sarvāstivādin path-architecture Malcolm deprioritizes. Its absence from Module C is structurally consistent with Malcolm's pedagogical choice; **leaving it unaddressed is the correct decision for this curriculum.** If you ever want to engage it, the Dharmajoti introduction in Sangpo Vol 1 and academic literature on the *daśajñāna* would be the entry points.

### Week 18 (Integration)

Week 18 is a review and integration week with no new chapter assigned. Sourced from re-reading and Malcolm's framing material from S1.C1. Fully grounded.

## Part IV — Operational Implications for Course Design

### For Weeks 1–14 (Vols 1–2)

You can proceed week-by-week with confidence: each week has a corresponding Malcolm class (or part of one) you can listen to before or during your study of the assigned verses. The reading-plus-transcript pairing is exactly what Module C's "Series Structure → 18-Week Map" promises, and the promise holds for these fourteen weeks.

**Suggested rhythm:** Read the assigned chapter verses first (root verses only, per Malcolm's own instruction). Listen to the corresponding Malcolm class. Re-read the verses with anything Malcolm highlighted in mind. Move into the week's contemplative practice and cross-tradition work. This rhythm aligns with the Recommended Weekly Practice Rhythm in the curriculum overview.

### For Weeks 15–17 (Vols 3–4)

Three options for handling the transcript-thin weeks:

**Option A — Defer to a future semester.** Take Module C as a 14-week curriculum (Vols 1–2 only) in the first semester. Develop a dedicated 4-week module for Vols 3–4 in a future semester, possibly paired with Mipham Rinpoche's *Beacon of Certainty* and academic scholarship (Dhammajoti, Pruden's introduction, the broader Vasubandhu literature). This is the most honest option pedagogically because it acknowledges that the transcript scaffold doesn't extend into these chapters.

**Option B — Lean on the Madhyamaka co-week.** Module C Weeks 15–17 are co-keyed to Madhyamaka Weeks 15–17, which themselves are well-developed in your revised Module B. Use the Madhyamaka analysis as the analytical backbone, treating the AKBh chapters as background source-material that the Madhyamaka analysis is engaging with rather than as primary readings in their own right. Module C Week 17's Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam confrontation is structurally the strongest part of the late Module C, and its primary engagement with PH sūtra 1 against AKBh Ch. 9 stands independently of Malcolm's coverage of Ch. 9\.

**Option C — Use academic secondary sources.** For Ch. 6 (path), the most useful secondary sources are Jay Garfield's treatments and the Kragh/Buswell Buddhist studies literature on the *ārya* taxonomy. For Ch. 8 (dhyānas), the standard reference is Lance Cousins's work on the *jhānas* in comparative perspective. For Ch. 9, both Duerlinger's *Indian Buddhist Studies on Non-Buddhist Theories of a Self* (in your Source Material folder) and James Duerlinger's separate translation/study of Ch. 9 are essential.

### My Recommendation

**Option A \+ B combined: take Module C as a 14-week curriculum for the first semester, then add a focused 4-week Vols 3–4 module either as the final phase of a second semester or as a freestanding short course.** Reasons:

1. The Module C revision's structural integrity is highest in Weeks 1–14, where every week has full transcript coverage. Weeks 15–17 are sound in design but operate on lighter transcript scaffolding than the rest.  
2. Malcolm's own pedagogical choice was to deprioritize Vols 3 content for Mahāyāna practitioners. Following his choice rather than overriding it is consistent with the canon hierarchy you established (Malcolm Smith's direct teachings at the top).  
3. The Madhyamaka curriculum (Module B) does the heavy philosophical work for Weeks 15–17 anyway — Śāntarakṣita's critique of non-Buddhist views, the Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam confrontation, the *prapañca* and *catuṣkoṭi* applied to the Tathāgata. The Module C Vols 3–4 material is the Abhidharmic substrate; the Madhyamaka work is the operative analysis.  
4. A focused future Vols 3–4 module can be designed in conversation with whatever Malcolm material emerges from the "Abhidharma Lectures with Malcolm Smith" folder (the audio collection beyond the two completed series). If Malcolm has given additional lectures specifically on Chs. 6–9 in other contexts, they would feed into that future module.

### Pacing Implication for the Master Curriculum

If Module C becomes a 14-week curriculum, the Master Curriculum integration (Module C running concurrently with Module B) shifts:

- **Module B (20 weeks)** continues as currently designed.  
- **Module C (14 weeks)** runs concurrently with Module B Weeks 1–14, then Module C completes while Module B continues for six more weeks (Weeks 15–20).  
- **The future Vols 3–4 module** could be designed to run during Module B's Weeks 15–18 in a subsequent semester, giving the late Madhyamaka work explicit Abhidharmic counterpart material at exactly the right structural moment.

This pacing is more honest to the source material than forcing 18 weeks of Module C content when only 14 are transcript-grounded.

## Part V — Future Semester Targets

When you design the future Vols 3–4 module, the following correlations are the natural entry points:

### Vol 3 / Ch. 6 (Mārga)

- **AKBh content:** Five paths, sixteen moments of path of seeing, seven *ārya* persons, four pairs of eight individuals, fetters  
- **Required reading:** Sangpo Vol 3, Ch. 6 (Mārgapudgalanirdeśa); about 700 pages but mostly verse-and-commentary; can be skim-read for the verses, deep-read for selected commentary  
- **Companion text:** Mipham Rinpoche's *Beacon of Certainty* on view and path  
- **Academic supplement:** Jay Garfield, *Engaging Buddhism*, chapter on the path  
- **Cross-tradition correlate:** Compare with Candrakīrti's *Madhyamakāvatāra* 10-*bhūmi* structure (your Module B Phase III) and the Dzogchen path-or-no-path question  
- **Open question target:** Is rebirth a doctrinal commitment, an empirical claim, or a structural device? Ch. 6's path structure presupposes rebirth (stream-enterer's seven lifetimes, never-returner's form-realm rebirth); how does this presupposition function in Mahāyāna and Dzogchen path-theory that has more flexible relationships to multi-life path-structure?

### Vol 3 / Ch. 7 (Jñāna)

- **AKBh content:** The ten cognitions, the qualities of the Buddha, the eighteen unshared qualities of a Buddha  
- **Required reading:** Sangpo Vol 3, Ch. 7 (Jñānanirdeśa); shorter than Ch. 6  
- **Academic supplement:** The Dhammajoti introduction in Sangpo Vol 1 covers the *daśajñāna* (ten cognitions) doctrine; the broader Sarvāstivāda *abhijñā* literature is the academic context  
- **Cross-tradition correlate:** Mahāyāna treatments of the Buddha's qualities (the *trikāya* schema, the *guṇa* literature); the Dzogchen account of *jñāna* (Tibetan: *ye shes*) as the natural radiance of *rigpa*  
- **Honest framing:** This chapter is the deepest into Sarvāstivādin doxography of any in the AKBh and is the most philosophically distant from your lineage. It may be appropriate to read it in survey rather than depth.

### Vol 3 / Ch. 8 (Samāpatti)

- **AKBh content:** Four *rūpa-dhyānas*, four formless absorptions (*ārūpya-samāpatti*), *nirodha-samāpatti*, the *kuśala-mūla*s, the entry conditions for each absorption  
- **Required reading:** Sangpo Vol 3, Ch. 8 (Samāpattinirdeśa)  
- **Companion text:** Patañjali's *Yoga Sūtras* Book III (Vibhūti Pāda) on *saṃyama* and the *siddhi*s — for comparative reading; not for adoption of Patañjali's *puruṣa*\-based metaphysics  
- **Academic supplement:** Lance Cousins's articles on *jhāna* in the Pāli vs. Sanskrit Buddhist traditions; Bhante Anālayo's recent work on the absorptions  
- **Cross-tradition correlate:** Module B Week 16 (your Madhyamaka curriculum) treats the dhyānas and *nirodha-samāpatti* in the broader Madhyamaka emptiness frame; this future Module C week would provide the Abhidharmic technical substrate  
- **YTT application:** The dhyāna-factor analysis (vitarka, vicāra, prīti, sukha, ekāgratā) is the most directly teachable Abhidharmic framework for your studio context — a precise vocabulary for what is happening in deepening concentration, applicable to āsana, prāṇāyāma, and seated meditation alike

### Vol 4 / Ch. 9 (Pudgalapratiṣedhaprakaraṇa)

- **AKBh content:** The full refutation of the Vātsīputrīya *pudgala*, the Brahmanical *ātman*, Īśvara as creator, the *jīva*/eternal soul; the fire-and-fuel simile in extended development; the conventional designation of person as *santāna*  
- **Required reading:** Sangpo Vol 4, Ch. 9 in full (the entire volume is just this chapter, about 600 pages of dense argumentation)  
- **Companion text:** Already established in Module B Week 17 — the Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam (PH sūtra 1\) confrontation  
- **Academic supplement:** James Duerlinger, *Indian Buddhist Theories of Persons* (his dedicated study of Ch. 9; in your Source Material folder); the broader Sastri/Sangpo introductions  
- **Cross-tradition correlate:** This chapter is the natural anchor of any future Process/Holopoeisis course as well, since the question of whether a non-Buddhist substantial absolute (Whitehead's *Creativity*, Whitehead's *God*) can survive Vasubandhu's Ch. 9 critique is the structural fork that determines the relationship between Process Philosophy and Mādhyamaka *anātman*

### Open Pedagogical Question for the Future Module

Whether to design the future Vols 3–4 module as a 4-week unit (one week per chapter, condensed) or as an 8-week unit (two weeks per chapter, with proper depth) depends on:

1. Whether additional Malcolm material on these chapters becomes available from the broader "Abhidharma Lectures with Malcolm Smith" folder  
2. Whether you've completed Module B Phase V by then, since the Madhyamaka analytical groundwork is what makes the Vols 3–4 reading productive  
3. Your overall semester load by that point

**A reasonable target: design the future module as 4 weeks if you have audio for the other chapters, 8 weeks if you're working primarily from text without additional Malcolm scaffolding.**
