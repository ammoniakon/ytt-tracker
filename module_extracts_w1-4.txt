MODULE A — WEEK 1
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  Malcolm Smith, trans., Yoga of the Natural State: The Dzogchen Aural Lineage
  (Wisdom Publications, 2025)
  — Translator's Introduction, pp. 1–12: from the opening through the end of
    "The Historical Context of the Great Perfection." Read slowly. Read twice.

Thematic focus:
  WHAT TO UNDERSTAND:
  (1) What distinguishes an aural lineage (nyan brgyud / rna brgyud) from a
      textual lineage (dpe brgyud) — this is the foundational structural claim
      of the entire book.
  (2) Longchenpa's triple classification of the intimate instruction series:
      outer, inner, and secret cycles, and where the unwritten aural lineage
      sits within the secret and unsurpassed secret cycles.
  (3) Malcolm Smith's argument that the Great Perfection is an independent
      tradition — not merely the culmination of Mahāyoga or the completion stage
      of Anuyoga — and how this bears on your own Anuyoga practice context.

  KEY PASSAGE TO MARK: The passage where the Introduction distinguishes the
  Great Perfection's basis of purification (ignorance / ma rig pa) from
  Mahāyoga's (the five aggregates, bases, and elements). This is foundational
  for YTT work: the Shaiva Tantric framework similarly treats the aggregates
  as the locus of transformation, and Dzogchen's radical departure from that
  approach will be a recurring critical tool.

  CONTEMPLATION: Malcolm notes that "the Great Perfection takes quite literally
  the Buddha's description of awakening… that buddhahood is peaceful,
  uncompounded, pure, free from all proliferation, and blissful." Sit with this.
  Ask yourself: is your current practice oriented toward generating something,
  or recognizing something already present? How does the uncompounded
  (asaṃskṛta) character of rigpa differ from the Śiva-consciousness of Kashmir
  Shaivism, which presents awakening as a recognition of an already-existing
  absolute Śiva-nature? Note your observations without resolving them — this
  tension will be with you all fourteen weeks.

  JOURNAL PROMPT: What is the relationship between hearing a teaching and knowing
  a teaching? Why would Malcolm Smith argue that the oral transmission is
  indispensable, not merely culturally quaint?

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Mod-B Wk 1: MMK Preface and VV Introduction — the self-refutation problem of
  emptiness. Where Madhyamaka opens with the question of how the claim of
  emptiness applies to itself, Dzogchen opens with the question of how the
  recognition of the natural state is transmitted if the natural state is itself
  uncompounded. Both are grappling with the same structural problem from different
  angles: how a non-substantial truth can be taught.

  Mod-C Wk 1: AKBh I.1–5 — the dharma-ontology question. Abhidharma's project
  of cataloguing dharmas presumes that the basic constituents of experience are
  analytically given; the aural lineage tradition's claim is that the basic given
  is not a dharma but a recognition. The tension: is the Abhidharma's exhaustive
  catalogue an aid or a hindrance to recognition? Vasubandhu's later
  Pudgala-pratiṣedha (Module C Week 13) will partially answer this; the aural
  lineage's answer is structurally different.

MODULE A — WEEK 2
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  Malcolm Smith, trans., Yoga of the Natural State
  — Translator's Introduction, pp. 12–33: from "The Mind Series Aural Lineage"
    through the end of the Introduction, including all footnotes relevant to the
    six aural lineages and the texts included in this volume.

Thematic focus:
  WHAT TO UNDERSTAND:
  (1) Map the three main aural lineage traditions: the mind series (Vimalamitra),
      the space series (Vajra Bridge / Namkhai Norbu), and the intimate instruction
      series (Longchenpa) — the subject of this book.
  (2) Understand the six aural lineage texts included in this volume and their
      relationship to the Lama Yangtik and Zabmo Yangtik cycles.
  (3) Understand the structural scheme organizing all texts: the six bardos and
      the six lamps, introduced by Longchenpa into Great Perfection literature
      in the fourteenth century.

  KEY PASSAGE TO MARK: Malcolm's explanation of the unwritten aural lineage:
  its dependence not on texts or formal empowerment but on direct pointing-out
  by a qualified guru, and the physiological rather than analytical basis for
  distinguishing sems (mind) from ye shes (pristine consciousness). This will
  become extremely important in Weeks 5–8.

  COMPREHENSION CHECK: Draw a simple diagram mapping the lineage:
  Samantabhadra → Vajrasattva → Garab Dorjé → Mañjuśrīmitra → Śrī Siṃha →
  Jñānasūtra → Vimalamitra → … → Kumarāja → Longchenpa. Observe that this is
  a lineage of direct oral transmission, not textual inheritance alone.

  CONTEMPLATION: The three texts in the Lama Yangtik are: (1) the short aural
  lineage, focused on the practice of empty vidyā in this life; (2) the
  middle-length aural lineage, focused on luminosity in the six bardos; (3) the
  great aural lineage, integrating both. Ask yourself honestly: which of the
  three calibers — best, middling, average — do you find yourself in most
  naturally? Not as a judgment but as a diagnostic for how to read the
  remainder of the book.

  PRACTICE: Continue daily guru yoga and natural state rest. This week, after
  settling, spend five minutes deliberately distinguishing sems (conceptual
  elaboration, discursive movement) from rig pa (naked non-conceptual awareness).
  Don't analyze — just notice whether there is a felt difference.

  JOURNAL PROMPT: Malcolm argues that the Great Perfection "eschews the kind of
  intellectual analysis Western attention is focused upon." How does this challenge
  or confirm your own approach to studying Dharma? How does it relate to the
  pramāṇa tradition and the role of inference vs. direct perception in your
  understanding of Madhyamaka?

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Mod-B Wk 2: MMK Ch. 1–2 (Causation, Motion). Dzogchen's lineage transmission
  is not a causal sequence in the sense Madhyamaka analyzes (cause produces
  effect). It is presented as a transmission of recognition, not a generation
  of new content. This is structurally compatible with Mādhyamika analysis of
  causation as empty of inherent productive activity.

  Mod-C Wk 2: AKBh I.6–47 — rūpa-skandha (matter, atoms, sense-fields).
  Abhidharma's atomic analysis of matter is the conventional-level framework
  that Dzogchen's subtle-body teaching (Weeks 5–11) will engage critically.
  The touchpoint: what is the body, analytically? Abhidharma offers atoms;
  Dzogchen offers nāḍī, vāyu, and bindu as the channels of pristine
  consciousness. The two analyses are not competing on the same level.

MODULE A — WEEK 3
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  Malcolm Smith, trans., Yoga of the Natural State
  — Self-Appearing Pristine Consciousness: The Short Aural Lineage,
    from the opening verse through Section 2.1.7 (The Yoga of the Natural State),
    approximately pages 33–38.

Thematic focus:
  WHAT TO UNDERSTAND:
  (1) Memorize or deeply familiarize yourself with the seven preliminary
      practices: (1) Guru Yoga, (2) Maṇḍala Yoga, (3) Vajrasattva, (4) Yoga of
      Impermanence, (5) Yoga of the Suffering of Saṃsāra, (6) Yoga of
      Bodhicitta, (7) Yoga of the Natural State.
  (2) Understand the structural logic of this preliminary sequence: why does
      the natural state investigation come last? What has been prepared by the
      six prior practices?
  (3) Pay close attention to the seventh yoga — the natural state investigation —
      and its classical fourfold analysis: Where does mind arise from? What is
      its producing agent? Where does it dwell? Where does it depart?

  KEY PASSAGE TO MARK AND WHY IT MATTERS FOR YTT:
  "Examine apparent objects for subtle particles. After the mind arises from an
  object, does it stay or go? Identify the entity by investigating the dwelling
  agent, a moment of one's mind — does it exist as a sign, mark, shape, or
  color? As nothing whatsoever can be found with a thorough investigation, the
  inexpressible, uniform state is called 'the transcendent state of original
  purity's own place.'"

  This passage is directly relevant to your YTT context. The fourfold
  investigation (origin, agent, dwelling, departure) is structurally identical
  to Vasubandhu's Abhidharmakośa analysis of momentary dharmas — but the
  purpose is diametrically opposed to Abhidharma: not to catalogue the dharmas
  of experience but to dissolve any referent for the investigator.

  FOR YOUR YTT: Your YTT likely teaches some form of body-scan, prāṇāyāma, or
  inquiry as preliminary practices. Note the parallel structure but the
  radically different endpoint: Shaiva Tantra's preliminaries orient practice
  toward the recognition of Śiva-consciousness as one's own nature (a universal
  self-recognition). Dzogchen's seventh yoga points toward the recognition that
  the investigator itself cannot be found — there is no ātman, universal or
  individual, to recover. The kalyāṇa (beneficent quality) of natural state
  investigation is precisely its non-finding.

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Mod-B Wk 3: MMK Ch. 3–5 (Āyatanas, Skandhas, Dhātus). Strong convergence
  this week: Nāgārjuna's analysis of the aggregates and Longchenpa's fourfold
  investigation of mind are doing the same work from different angles. Nāgārjuna
  asks where the aggregate is when analyzed; Longchenpa asks where the mind
  that examines the aggregate is. Both arrive at non-finding.

  Mod-C Wk 3: AKBh I.5–15 — the five aggregates and unconditioned dharmas.
  The conventional-level catalogue against which Madhyamaka's emptiness analysis
  and Dzogchen's natural-state investigation are both operating. Hold the three
  frameworks in active relationship this week: the aggregates as Abhidharma
  analyzes them are conventionally real; as Madhyamaka analyzes them they are
  empty of inherent existence; as Longchenpa investigates them they cannot be
  found by the investigator who is also empty.

MODULE A — WEEK 4
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  Malcolm Smith, trans., Yoga of the Natural State
  — Self-Appearing Pristine Consciousness: The Short Aural Lineage,
    Sections 2.2 through 3 (The Conclusion), approximately pages 38–50.

Thematic focus:
  WHAT TO UNDERSTAND:
  (1) The two-part structure of the direct introduction: (a) introducing
      self-abiding consciousness (rang bzhin gnas pa'i shes pa) as unfabricated,
      and (b) introducing self-arising consciousness (rang shar gyi shes pa) as
      great self-liberation.
  (2) The three examples, five natures, and four signs of heat for each
      introduction — the technical scaffolding of the practice instruction.
  (3) The conduct section (2.2.1.3): training in the sixfold group during
      equipoise, and the eight examples of illusion in post-equipoise.

  KEY PASSAGES TO MARK AND MEMORIZE:
  The five natures of the first introduction: "dwell in that state, maintain it
  with mindfulness, do not be moved by concepts, eliminate hope and fear, and
  annul lethargy or agitation." These are the five key points of trekchö
  practice summarized in a single sentence.

  The three examples for the second introduction: waves dissolving into a river
  (self-liberation of play), ripples on water (vanishing without a trace),
  coils of a snake (self-liberation on its own). These classic similes recur
  throughout the Nyingma literature. Internalize them.

  COMPREHENSION CHECK: Explain the difference between "self-abiding
  consciousness" and "self-arising consciousness." Why are both needed? What
  is the danger of attaining the first without the second? (The first risks
  falling into a fixed, blank stillness — jing ne — without the dynamic
  recognition of self-liberation in movement.)

  TECHNICAL ANALYSIS: The four signs of heat for the second introduction
  include: "Since concepts do not harm the stilled mind, conceptuality and
  nonconceptuality are the same." This is the Dzogchen resolution of the
  classical Mahāyāna tension between śamatha and vipaśyanā — not a progressive
  integration, but a simultaneous recognition. Compare this with Nāgārjuna's
  Vigrahavyāvartanī: the absence of inherent existence in phenomena does not
  require abandoning their appearance. The appearances arise; recognition
  liberates them. No separate antidote is needed.

  PRACTICE LAYER: Using the text as a literal practice guide, formally attempt
  the first introduction: relax body and mind; rest in vidyā's unceasing
  radiance, applying the three examples, then the five natures. Then attempt
  the second: rest in the limpid state (ngang dwangs), and watch whatever
  concepts arise — introducing them as self-arising and self-liberated using
  the three examples.

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Mod-B Wk 4: MMK Ch. 6–9 (the analysis of agent, action, and substance).
  Nāgārjuna's analysis of the relationship between agent and action lands
  precisely on the question Dzogchen's two introductions address: when there
  is no agent who acts and no action that is acted, what is recognition?
  Madhyamaka analyzes the absence; Dzogchen rests in the recognition that is
  the absence's positive aspect.

  Mod-C Wk 4: AKBh II — saṃskāra-skandha and citta / cittaviprayukta-saṃskāra.
  The conventional analysis of mental formations and mind. The touchpoint:
  Abhidharma's "mental factors" are precisely what Dzogchen's second introduction
  asks the practitioner to see as self-arising and self-liberated. The Abhidharma
  categorizes; the Dzogchen practice releases.

MODULE B — WEEK 1
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  (a) Nāgārjuna, Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (MMK) — Preface and Introduction
      (Siderits & Katsura, 2013, pp. ix–15)
  (b) Nāgārjuna, Vigrahavyāvartanī (VV) — Introduction (Westerhoff, 2010, pp. 1–30)
  (c) Geshe Sopa, Nāgārjuna's Advice for Buddhists (GSN) — Chapter 1,
      "Overview of the Middle Way," through Geshe Sopa's opening commentary

Thematic focus:
  THEMATIC FOCUS: What does it mean to assert that all things are empty (śūnya)
  of intrinsic nature (svabhāva)? The central distinction from the opening
  movement is this: emptiness is not the claim that things do not exist, but
  that they do not exist from their own side — independently, permanently, or
  self-sufficiently. Siderits & Katsura's introduction clarifies that the MMK's
  27 chapters constitute a sustained philosophical demonstration of this single
  thesis across domains of causation, selfhood, time, motion, and liberation.
  Westerhoff's introduction to the VV situates the epistemological stakes: if
  Nāgārjuna's own words have no intrinsic nature, does his argument undermine
  itself? This self-refutation objection is the problem the VV addresses, and
  encountering it in week one forces immediate engagement with the recursive
  dimension of emptiness-talk.

  CONTEMPLATIVE PRACTICE FOCUS: Begin sitting with the question: What do I
  assume is "just there" — present, stable, self-existing — in my experience
  right now? Notice the tacit assumption of a perceiver who stands apart from
  perceived contents. This is not yet a Madhyamaka analysis; it is an
  orientation toward what the analysis will eventually disrupt.

  YOGA TEACHING APPLICATION: In opening a class, consider how the cue "arrive
  in your body" or "come home to yourself" implicitly posits a self that travels
  and a home that awaits. This week's practice is simply noticing those
  assumptions. You need not revise the language yet — noticing is the first
  layer of teaching. A possible cue that begins to soften: "Let attention settle
  into sensations arising right now — not to a place, but into the dynamic flow
  of what is actually present."

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Abhidharma Pairing: AKBh Ch. 1 on dharmas — the Abhidharma's atomistic
  decomposition of experience into irreducible constituents provides the
  philosophical baseline Nāgārjuna will interrogate. The Vaibhāṣika claim that
  dharmas possess svabhāva in the intrinsic-nature sense is precisely what
  Madhyamaka refutes. Holding both readings simultaneously — Abhidharma's
  conventionally necessary categories and Madhyamaka's ultimate analysis —
  trains the two-truth lens from the start.

  Sitting question (Shaiva interlocutor): In the YTT context, Śiva-consciousness
  is posited as the ground of all appearances. PH sūtra 1 (Singh, p. 47) states:
  "The absolute citi (Consciousness), out of its own free will, is the cause of
  the siddhi of the universe." What would it mean for that citi to also be empty
  of svabhāva? Is that a destabilizing thought or a liberating one?

MODULE B — WEEK 2
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  (a) MMK: Ch. 1 "An Analysis of Conditions" and Ch. 2 "An Analysis of the
      Traversed, the Not Yet Traversed, and the Presently Being Traversed"
      (Siderits & Katsura, pp. 17–40)
  (b) GSN: Geshe Sopa's commentary on dependent origination as the heart of
      the middle way

Thematic focus:
  THEMATIC FOCUS: Chapter 1 is arguably the most densely analytical chapter in
  the entire text, and beginning here is non-negotiable. Nāgārjuna examines the
  four conditions recognized by Buddhist Abhidharma — causal condition
  (hetu-pratyaya), object condition (ālambanapratyaya), immediately preceding
  condition (samanantarapratyaya), and dominant condition (adhipatipratyaya) —
  and argues that none of them can explain how effects arise from their own side.
  Effects do not pre-exist in causes, nor do they arise from nothing; they arise
  dependently (pratītyasamutpāda). Chapter 2 applies this logic to motion: the
  traversed path, the not-yet traversed, and the presently-being-traversed each
  fail, under analysis, to sustain the concept of motion as an independently real
  process.

  CONTEMPLATIVE PRACTICE FOCUS: Sit with a single breath. Notice: does the
  breath exist before it arises? Does it exist after it ceases? Where exactly
  is the "breath" — as an independent, intrinsically real event? This is not
  rhetorical; let it be a genuine phenomenological inquiry.

  YOGA TEACHING APPLICATION: Sequence logic — the idea that one pose causes
  the next — often carries a hidden substantialist assumption: that the first
  pose establishes a "real" state that is then transferred to the second. A
  Madhyamaka re-framing: "Notice how each shape arises out of the conditions
  of the previous one — nothing is fixed in place, each position is already
  releasing into what comes next." This dissolves the implicit fixation on
  "achieving" a pose as a stable end-state.

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Abhidharma Pairing: AK Ch. 2 on causation (hetupratyaya). The Vaibhāṣika
  enumeration of causes and conditions is the direct target of MMK Ch. 1. Reading
  them side by side illuminates what Nāgārjuna is refusing: not the convention of
  causation, but any theory that grants causes or conditions intrinsic causal power.

  Sitting question (Shaiva interlocutor): MMK 1 attacks the Abhidharma notion
  that dharmas have causal power from their own side. Does the Shaiva Tantra
  notion of Śakti as the dynamic power of Śiva survive this analysis? PH sūtra 2
  (Singh, p. 49) gives the Trika position: "By the power of her own free will,
  she [citi] unfolds the universe on her own screen." What would Nāgārjuna's
  logic imply for this claim that the universe arises by citi's autonomous power?

MODULE B — WEEK 3
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  (a) MMK: Ch. 3 "An Analysis of the Āyatanas," Ch. 4 "An Analysis of the
      Skandhas," Ch. 5 "An Analysis of the Dhātus" (Siderits & Katsura, pp. 41–73)
  (b) LF: Opening verses and Kangyur Rinpoche's commentary on the six sense
      spheres and the absence of a possessor

Thematic focus:
  THEMATIC FOCUS: These three short but potent chapters attack the Abhidharma's
  conventional categories from the inside. The twelve āyatanas (sense bases),
  the five skandhas (aggregates), and the eighteen dhātus (elements of
  experience) are the Abhidharma's decomposition of what ordinary thought takes
  to be a "self." Nāgārjuna does not reject these categories as conventionally
  valid; he demonstrates that they cannot be understood as intrinsically real
  even by Abhidharma's own logic. Crucially for your practice context: the
  skandhas are the body-mind complex precisely as it appears in yogic inquiry —
  rūpa (form/body), vedanā (sensation-tone), saṃjñā (perception), saṃskāra
  (volition/formations), vijñāna (consciousness). MMK Ch. 4's analysis of these
  as lacking svabhāva is a direct interlocutor to the YTT teaching that the
  body is a field of Śiva-consciousness.

  CONTEMPLATIVE PRACTICE FOCUS: In formal sitting, move through a body scan
  framed not as "feeling into my body" (possessive self-assumption) but as
  "noticing the arising and passing of form-sensations without locating a center
  from which noticing occurs." Let the āyatana analysis become phenomenological.

  YOGA TEACHING APPLICATION: The five-kośa model (annamaya, prāṇamaya, manomaya,
  vijñānamaya, ānandamaya) that appears in many YTT curricula maps loosely onto
  the skandhas and operates with the same risk: that the "sheaths" are real
  containers of a self within them. A Madhyamaka cue: "As you move through this
  form, notice that what we call 'the body' is a dynamic process — sensation
  arising, shifting, dissolving — without a fixed center that owns the movement."

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Abhidharma Pairing: AK Chs. 1–2 on the dharma lists and the
  skandha-āyatana-dhātu taxonomy. This is the most direct pairing in the
  curriculum: read the Abhidharma's positive account of these categories alongside
  Nāgārjuna's negative analysis of them. The contrast illuminates both.

  Sitting question: If the skandhas have no svabhāva individually, does the
  aggregate person have any more or less reality than its parts? What does
  Candrakīrti's analysis of the chariot (MA Ch. 6) anticipate here?

  Sitting question (somatic context): In somatic yoga therapy, practitioners
  often speak of "releasing held patterns in the body." What is the implicit
  theory of self in that language? Can it be reframed through the skandha
  analysis?

MODULE B — WEEK 4
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  (a) MMK: Ch. 6 "Desire and the One Who Desires," Ch. 7 "The Conditioned,"
      Ch. 8 "Object and Agent," Ch. 9 "What Is Prior," Ch. 10 "Fire and Fuel"
      (Siderits & Katsura, pp. 75–130)
  (b) VV: Translation of the root verses with Westerhoff's commentary
      (full text, pp. 30–100)

Thematic focus:
  THEMATIC FOCUS: These chapters constitute Nāgārjuna's sustained analysis of
  relational identity — the way we speak of agent and action, seer and seen,
  enjoyer and enjoyed as though they stood in determinate, intrinsic relations.
  Chapter 10's fire-fuel analysis is among the most elegant: fire and fuel cannot
  be identical (since fire would burn itself) nor entirely distinct (since fire
  without fuel is nothing). The relationship between them has no intrinsic nature;
  it is dependently constituted. This logic directly parallels the Shaiva Tantra
  teaching on Śiva and Śakti — but with a crucial divergence: for Nāgārjuna, the
  relational constitution of fire-and-fuel does not produce a higher-order
  substance (Śiva as absolute ground); it produces śūnyatā. This is where careful
  inter-traditional dialogue must be most precise. The VV's self-refutation
  discussion deepens this: Nāgārjuna's words themselves arise dependently — they
  have the epistemic force they do not because they intrinsically possess it, but
  through their dependent relationship with the philosophical context that makes
  them intelligible.

  CONTEMPLATIVE PRACTICE FOCUS: Sit with the question: Am I the one who is
  meditating, or is meditation arising? Notice the tendency to install an agent
  behind the activity. Let the agent-action distinction soften without dissolving
  into confusion.

  YOGA TEACHING APPLICATION: The prāṇa model in Shaiva Tantra posits prāṇa as
  the dynamic power of consciousness. When we teach "direct your prāṇa," we
  implicitly posit a directing agent who is distinct from the directed prāṇa.
  A Madhyamaka-informed cue: "Rather than directing the breath, let the breath
  be noticed — the distinction between breather and breath softening into the
  single event of breathing."

Tensions / cross-refs:
  Abhidharma Pairing: AK Ch. 9 on the pudgala — the Pudgalavādin's claim that
  there is a real "person" (pudgala) that is neither identical to nor different
  from the skandhas. Nāgārjuna's MMK Ch. 6–10 arguments apply equally well
  against this quasi-substantialist position, making the pairing doctrinally
  precise.

  Sitting question (Trika interlocutor): The fire-fuel analysis bears directly
  on the Trika's Śiva-Śakti relation. PH sūtra 4 (Singh, p. 60): "The individual
  [experient], in whom citi or consciousness is contracted, has the universe [as
  his body] in a contracted form." The contracted experient is neither identical
  with citi (since citi is unlimited) nor entirely different (since the
  contraction is citi's own saṅkoca). How does Nāgārjuna's fire-fuel logic apply
  to this Śiva-Śakti relation as Kṣemarāja sets it up?

  Sitting question (Dzogchen resonance): How does the VV's response to the
  self-refutation objection relate to Dzogchen's claim that rigpa is
  self-validating (rang gi rig pa)? Is there a tension between Madhyamaka's
  dissolution of epistemic foundations and Dzogchen's confident assertion of the
  self-knowing quality of awareness?

MODULE C — WEEK 1
================================================================================

Primary reading:
  Vasubandhu, Abhidharmakośa-Bhāṣya (AKBh)
  Gelong Lodrö Sangpo trans. (Motilal, 2012), Vol. 1
  Malcolm's Class: Series 1, Class 1 | AKBh Chapter 1 (Dhātunirdeśa): Introduction

  — Root verses: I.1–5
  — Sangpo Vol. 1: Prefatory material, Introduction by Poussin (trans. Sangpo), pp. xix–lv
  — Root verses I.1–5 (the three jewels opening; definition of Abhidharma)
  — Commentary on I.1–5

Thematic focus:
  CORE DOCTRINAL TOPICS:
  · What does 'Abhidharma' mean? Three senses: (1) pure wisdom (prajñā),
    (2) its attendant dharmas, (3) the śāstra itself
  · The five inner sciences (adhyātmavidyā) and Abhidharma's place among them
  · Vasubandhu as author: Kashmiri Vaibhāṣika presentation with Sautrāntika critique

Five-part analysis — YTT Module 1 (Subtle Bodies):

  1. WHAT THE YTT TEACHES:
  The YTT manual opens with the pañcamaya kosha system — five energetic sheaths
  from gross to subtle: annamaya (physical/food body), prāṇamaya (energetic/breath
  body), manomaya (emotional/mental body), vijñānamaya (intellectual/psychic body),
  and ānandamaya (bliss/spiritual body). The manual explicitly identifies the
  ānandamaya kosha as Ātman, the Self, God, or Universal Consciousness — the
  innermost reality that the other sheaths conceal and that yoga practice
  progressively reveals. The manual states directly: "By working to balance all
  five kosha bodies, yogis can experience the ānandamaya kosha. This is Ātman,
  the Self, God, or Universal Consciousness." The chakra system (seven centers
  from mūlādhāra to sahasrāra) is presented as a map of energetic concentrations
  along the suṣumnā nāḍī. Prāṇa is described as a universal life-force drawn
  from oxygen, food, sunlight, relationships. The aura is presented as the
  outermost manifestation of tejas (refined prāṇa). The teaching arc is clear:
  yoga practice moves energy from gross to subtle, ultimately culminating in the
  experience of Ātman / Universal Consciousness. The Yoga Sūtras reading
  reinforces this: Patañjali's definition of yoga implies a Seer (draṣṭā, Puruṣa)
  who is revealed when the fluctuations cease; Satchidananda's commentary
  identifies this Seer with the eternal, unchanging Self.

  2. WHAT THE CANON AFFIRMS:
  On prāṇa as dependently arising process: The YTT's description of prāṇa as a
  process that "animates" the body rather than a fixed entity resonates with
  Abhidharma's account of the prāṇendriya (vital faculty) as a conditioned,
  momentary dharma that sustains the body's activities. Vasubandhu does not treat
  the vital faculty as a permanent substance but as a causally conditioned stream.
  Similarly, the AKBh's analysis of rūpa-skandha treats the body not as a unified
  substantial entity but as a dynamic, causally interdependent network of momentary
  material dharmas.

  On the phenomenological value of the kosha model: The kosha system as a map —
  not as an ontological claim — has genuine diagnostic value. The YTT's use of it
  to structure somatic and therapeutic inquiry (which kosha is affected? where is
  the dis-ease showing up?) aligns with the Abhidharma's own phenomenological
  project: reduce your experience to its constituents for the purpose of insight,
  not because the categories are ultimately real. Read this way, the koshas are
  what Madhyamaka calls a saṃvṛti (conventional) designation — useful for
  navigation without being ultimately real.

  On the Dzogchen resonance with turīya: Chögyal Namkhai Norbu's teaching on the
  three kāyas (trikāya) as the three dimensions of the basis (gzhi) —
  dharmakāya as the open dimension, saṃbhogakāya as clarity, nirmāṇakāya as
  display — has structural parallels with the AUM/turīya model. Namkhai Norbu is
  explicit, however, that the Dzogchen recognition of rigpa is not the same as
  the Vedāntic recognition of Ātman: rigpa is not a Self, not a permanent subject,
  not Universal Consciousness in the ātman-sense.

  On the five elements: The pañca-mahābhūta as appearing in the YTT and the
  Abhidharma's four primary elements both treat the elements as qualities of
  experience rather than metaphysical substances. This is a point of genuine
  convergence: neither framework treats "earth" as a thing, but as a quality of
  sensory experience (solidity, resistance).

  3. WHERE THERE'S TENSION:
  The ātman/anātman fault line — the central tension of the entire YTT:
  The YTT manual states that the ānandamaya kosha is Ātman, the Self, God, or
  Universal Consciousness. This is a precise Vedāntic claim. It is the claim that
  Śāntarakṣita's Madhyamakālaṃkāra (Phase IV, Weeks 13–14 of the Madhyamaka
  curriculum) most directly refutes. Śāntarakṣita's systematic critique of
  Sāṃkhya's puruṣa and Vedānta's ātman — permanent, self-luminous consciousness-
  principles — applies with full force here. The crucial question: is the ānandamaya
  kosha (a) genuinely distinct from the other koshas (in which case it is other-
  natured, parabhāva, and cannot be intrinsically real per MMK 15); or (b)
  identical with them (in which case it is just the aggregates under another name)?

  The Trika alternative and its precise structural difference: The
  Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam offers a more sophisticated alternative. Kṣemarāja holds
  that citi (consciousness, the same as Śiva-nature) is the cause of the
  universe's manifestation (sūtra 1), and that this citi is self-luminous
  (svaprakāśa) and self-aware (vimarśa-rūpā). The contraction (saṅkoca) of this
  universal consciousness into limited individual consciousness produces the
  appearance of separate selves and a world of objects. This is more refined than
  the kosha-Ātman identification, but the structural problem from a Mādhyamika
  perspective is exactly the same — and arguably worse, because it is more
  philosophically robust. The citi of Pratyabhijñā has svabhāva: self-luminosity,
  self-awareness, autonomy (svātantrya). Candrakīrti's Prāsaṅgika analysis denies
  that consciousness can have svabhāva — denies, in particular, svasaṃvedana
  (consciousness's reflexive self-knowing), which is the very functional core of
  Kṣemarāja's citi.

  The container metaphor: The YTT uses the "Russian nesting doll" image — the
  physical body as the innermost doll, the spiritual body as the outermost
  container. This spatial metaphor implies literal containment: the Ātman as the
  largest, most real sheath that contains all others. This is a substantialist
  metaphor: it implies the sheaths have stable boundaries, endure through time,
  and are independently locatable. The Abhidharma's own decomposition of the body
  into momentary, causally conditioned dharmic streams gives no support to a
  "sheath" that persists with determinate properties across time.

  Prāṇa as substance vs. process: The YTT treats prāṇa as storable, transferable,
  depletable — a quasi-material energy substance. "Prenatal prāṇa" given by the
  mother, "postnatal prāṇa" drawn from food and sunlight — this is clearly a
  substance-model, not a process-model. The Buddhist parallel: prāṇendriya (vital
  faculty) is not a substance but a causally conditioned stream of moments, each
  arising and passing, each dependent on conditions. Wangyal's Bön/Dzogchen
  account of rlung is structurally process-compatible: rlung is not a substance
  but a quality of conditioned arising, the dynamism of the mind-body system,
  which is why it can be "ridden" by both confusion (karma rlung) and pristine
  cognition (ye shes rlung) without itself being a thing.

  Chakras as map vs. real structures: The YTT presents chakras as real energetic
  centers correlated with nerve plexuses, capable of being "activated," "blocked,"
  or "balanced." From the Madhyamaka two-truths framework (Phase II, Week 8), the
  chakras might be understood as saṃvṛti-satya — functional designations that
  organize energetic experience reliably, not intrinsically real structures with
  svabhāva. The problem is that the YTT treats them as real in the stronger sense,
  particularly when it claims kuṇḍalinī rises through them as it pierces each one.

  The Sāṃkhya substrate in Patañjali: Satchidananda's Yoga Sūtras commentary
  presents Patañjali's yoga through a broadly Vedāntic lens, but the Yoga Darśana
  is technically dualist — Puruṣa and Prakṛti are permanently distinct. The YTT
  manual (which conflates Vedāntic non-dualism with Patañjali's dualism) and the
  Satchidananda commentary (which assimilates both to Vedānta) are philosophically
  inconsistent with each other, and both are inconsistent with Buddhist anātman.

  4. REFRAME — THROUGH YOUR LINEAGE'S FRAMEWORK:
  The koshas as conventional phenomenological map: In your teaching, the kosha
  system can be retained as a saṃvṛti (conventional) diagnostic map — a useful
  way to help students tune attentional resolution from gross to subtle, from
  physical sensation to breath-tone to mental quality to discriminative awareness.
  The reframe is not that "the koshas don't exist" but that they do not exist from
  their own side. They are process-clusters, not containers. A reframed teaching:
  "We move attention from coarser to subtler textures of experience — not traveling
  from one layer to another as if crossing a border, but noticing that what we
  call 'the body' and what we call 'the breath' and what we call 'awareness' are
  not separate things, but different resolutions of a single dynamic process
  arising right now."

  Dzogchen reframe of the ānandamaya kosha: Where the YTT teaches that the
  ānandamaya kosha is Ātman, your lineage offers a different pointer: what is
  recognized in the deepest stillness is not a Self (which would imply a permanent
  subject distinct from its objects), but the gzhi (basis) of the natural state —
  open, luminous, and unimpeded, but not a "self" in any substantialist sense.
  Chögyal Namkhai Norbu is precise here: rigpa is not the same as Universal
  Consciousness in the Vedāntic sense. It is awareness without a center, luminous
  without a locus. The cue: "What remains when the need to locate a self in
  experience dissolves? Not emptiness as absence — but an open, luminous quality
  that was never a thing to begin with."

  Prāṇa reframe — interoception as the secular bridge: In teaching contexts, prāṇa
  can be reframed as a designative term for what we experience as the vitality of
  conditioned arising — the felt aliveness of the body is the body functioning as
  a dependently arising process. It is not a substance that fills us like water in
  a vessel. Murphy & Brewer's interoception literature gives you the bridge: "felt
  sense of body state" is exactly what prāṇa designates phenomenologically, without
  the metaphysical claim. A teaching cue: "What we call 'prāṇa' is not something
  you have — it is the quality of this moment of arising, right now, this breath,
  this heartbeat. When conditions are present, vitality arises. When conditions
  cease, it ceases. This isn't frightening — it's how a conditioned process works."

  Chakras as phenomenological anchors: Teach chakras as attentional foci — places
  where the mapping of awareness onto the body reveals patterns, textures, and
  tendencies — without committing to their intrinsic existence as real energetic
  structures. "When we bring attention to the region of the heart, what arises?
  Not because a 'heart chakra' is a fixed structure there, but because attention
  finds certain qualities of experience more available in certain regions. The map
  is useful. The map is not the territory."

  5. OPEN QUESTIONS:
  · The YTT equates ānandamaya kosha with Ātman and then with Brahman / Universal
    Consciousness. But the Taittirīya Upaniṣad's original teaching (from which the
    five-kosha model derives) treats the ānandamaya as the subtlest sheath — not
    as Ātman itself, which transcends all sheaths. Is the YTT manual accurately
    representing the Taittirīya teaching, or conflating Ātman with the last sheath?
    This matters: if Ātman transcends all koshas, it is an even stronger
    substantialist claim than if it is identified with one of them.
  · The Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam sūtras 1–4 present citi as both immanent in and
    transcendent to the manifest world. Is the Trika position a "stronger" or
    "weaker" form of substantialism than the YTT's kosha-Ātman identification?
    How does this affect the strategic question of where to focus critical
    attention?
  · The Abhidharma teaches avijñapti-rūpa: the taking of vows materially alters
    the aggregate. This is a striking parallel to the YTT claim that practice
    transforms the subtle bodies. Malcolm Smith notes this is rejected by
    Sautrāntikas and Mādhyamikas — but the Vajrayāna is not a Sautrāntika system.
    How does Vajrayāna account for the transformation of the body through practice
    (e.g., the vajrakāya) without importing substantialist assumptions?
  · Iyengar's framing of yoga as the integration of "the whole man" maps closely
    onto the kosha system but with a more embodied, less hierarchical emphasis.
    Where does Iyengar's commitment to a real, benefitting Self (jīvātman) show
    up most clearly in his practice instructions?
  · Nicholson's cognitive-scientific framing of anātman lets you translate
    Buddhist non-self vocabulary into terms a secular student can engage. Where
    does this translation lose something essential? (The hardest case: anātman
    is not the same as "the self is a brain process." The former dissolves the
    question of substrate; the latter affirms a different substrate.)

Five-part analysis — YTT Module 2 (Ayurveda):

  1. WHAT THE YTT TEACHES:
  Ayurveda, presented as the sister science of yoga, is built on the Vedas and
  structured through three major conceptual frameworks: the guṇas (tamas, rajas,
  sattva — inertia, activity, clarity); the tattvas (24 elements of manifestation
  from Brahman through the five gross elements); and the tridoṣic system (vāta,
  pitta, kapha — three bodily energies derived from the five elements). Disease
  is understood as an imbalance in these systems, and health as their
  harmonization. The manual presents prakṛti (one's natural constitution) and
  vikṛti (one's current imbalanced state) as clinical assessment tools. The
  tattva section explicitly names Brahman and "universal intelligence" among the
  24+ tattvas, framing manifestation as a hierarchical emanation from a single
  ground. The guṇas are treated as real qualities that exist in food, bodily
  tissue, environments, and relationships — and that can be assessed, modified,
  and balanced through diet, yoga, and vibrational attunement.

  2. WHAT THE CANON AFFIRMS:
  On diagnostic categories as conventional tools: The Abhidharma's own taxonomic
  project — dharma-lists, mental factor maps, elemental categories — is similarly
  motivated: provide a precise phenomenological vocabulary that can be used for
  diagnosis and practice. The tridoṣic system and the mahābhūmika mental factor
  chart are both conventional maps serving analogous functions. Malcolm Smith's
  teaching that "Abhidharma is like bug-checking the software of your mind"
  applies equally to Ayurvedic clinical assessment. The value is diagnostic, not
  ontological.

  On the five elements: The AKBh's four primary elements (earth/solidity,
  water/cohesion, fire/heat, air/motility) correspond closely to Ayurveda's five
  (with the addition of ākāśa/space). More importantly, both traditions treat the
  elements as qualities of experience — ways that bodily phenomena present
  themselves to awareness — rather than as metaphysical substances. Wangyal's
  Vajrayāna five-element teaching shares this phenomenological orientation while
  explicitly framing the elements as appearances of mind rather than as primary
  constituents of an external world.

  On the fire-and-fuel logic and pitta: Nāgārjuna's fire-fuel analysis (MMK Ch. 10)
  applies directly to Ayurveda's pitta: pitta is associated with fire and water,
  and governs digestion as transformative heat. The fire-fuel analysis demonstrates
  that fire and fuel cannot be intrinsically related — the transformation (pitta's
  digestive function) is a dependently arising process, not an intrinsic power.
  This supports the Ayurvedic clinical picture (the process of transformation is
  real at the conventional level) while refusing the ontological commitment to
  pitta as an independently real entity with svabhāva.

  3. WHERE THERE'S TENSION:
  The guṇas as svabhāva-claims: This is the most philosophically precise tension
  in the entire module. The guṇas in Sāṃkhya-Yoga are understood as real
  constitutive properties of Prakṛti — tamas, rajas, and sattva are the actual
  structural constituents of matter-energy-mind. Everything that exists (except
  Puruṣa) is made of guṇas in various proportions. This is a substantialist
  metaphysics: properties inhere in substances and are intrinsically possessed
  by them. MMK Ch. 15 refutes svabhāva with this precision: if sattva is
  intrinsically sattva (i.e., clarity is really, from its own side, clarity),
  then it cannot transform into rajas (activity). But Ayurveda's clinical model
  depends on the guṇas transforming into each other — pratipakṣa bhāvanā works
  by substituting one guṇa for another. If they can transform, they are not
  intrinsically what they are. If they are not intrinsically what they are, they
  lack svabhāva. The Sāṃkhya-Ayurvedic framework cannot have it both ways.

  The tattva system and emanation ontology: The Sāṃkhyan 24-tattva scheme and
  the Trika 36-tattva scheme both have three problems from the Madhyamaka
  perspective: (1) they posit an unconditioned ground (Brahman in Sāṃkhya,
  Śiva-tattva in Trika) with svabhāva; (2) the emanation relation is a causal
  claim, but MMK Ch. 1 argues that nothing can arise from an intrinsically real
  cause with intrinsic causal power; (3) both models treat manifestation as a
  departure from the real — gross matter is less real than the subtle tattvas.
  Madhyamaka flatly denies this ontological hierarchy.

  Prakṛti vs. vikṛti and the problem of an enduring constitution: The Ayurvedic
  clinical distinction assumes that there is a stable, enduring "natural" state
  that defines what the person really is. This assumes temporal continuity of a
  self-nature across time. The Abhidharma alternative: there is no single "natural
  constitution" that endures — there is a relational, moment-by-moment arising
  of the organism in its environment, shaped by its causal history. Saṃskāras
  (latent dispositions) account for the conventional appearance of stable
  constitutional types without granting intrinsic natures to them.

  4. REFRAME — THROUGH YOUR LINEAGE'S FRAMEWORK:
  Guṇas as process-descriptors: Retain the guṇas as experiential quality-
  descriptors — ways of characterizing the texture of arising moments. Rather
  than "tamas is a quality your body has," frame it: "Right now, the quality of
  arising is heavy, slow, resistant — what we call tamasic. This is not a property
  of a self; it is a quality of this moment of arising. And qualities are
  relational — what is tamasic relative to one condition is rajasic relative to
  another." This is Madhyamaka-consistent: guṇas as conventional designations
  functioning reliably without intrinsic nature.

  Tattvas as conventional map: In teaching, the tattva system can be used as a
  contemplative map from gross to subtle — a structured inquiry into the layers
  of experience — without requiring commitment to emanation ontology. Frame the
  tattvas as "a traditional model for moving attention from the coarser to the
  subtler textures of experience — a map, not a cosmological claim."

  Dis-ease as conditioned process: The Ayurvedic disease etiology — imbalance
  arising from accumulation, aggravation, and overflow of doṣic qualities — can
  be reframed as a process account: disease arises when conditions accumulate that
  generate systemic imbalance; health is restored when conditions are altered to
  support balance. A teaching cue: "Healing doesn't fix a broken person — it
  creates conditions for a process to move differently."

  Five elements as five wisdoms: Drawing on Module A Week 3 and Wangyal's
  Healing with Form, Energy, and Light: in their pure aspect, the five elements
  appear as the five wisdoms (pañca-jñāna). The earth element recognized in its
  nature is the mirror-like wisdom; water as discriminating wisdom; fire as
  equality wisdom; air as all-accomplishing wisdom; space as dharmadhātu wisdom.
  This is the Vajrayāna alternative to the Ayurvedic clinical framing — both are
  useful, neither requires substance commitments.

  5. OPEN QUESTIONS:
  · The Yoga Sūtras' account of the guṇas (as Sāṃkhya's real constituents of
    Prakṛti) and the YTT's account of the guṇas (as Ayurvedic clinical
    categories) use the same words for different ontological commitments. Is there
    a way to use guṇa-language clinically without importing the Sāṃkhya
    metaphysics?
  · Malcolm Smith notes that in Vajrayāna, the five elements appear as five
    wisdoms in their pure aspect (the "five lights" of the natural state). How
    does this differ from Ayurveda's use of the five elements as constitutional
    factors? The key question: do the five wisdoms require a permanent ground-
    consciousness (Śiva or ātman) to be "pure," or are they already pure as
    dependently arisen events?
  · The Sautrāntika critique of the AKBh's elemental analysis: the four primary
    elements as momentary qualities, not substances. Apply this to the Ayurvedic
    claim that doṣas accumulate over time. Is there a moment-by-moment process
    account of doṣic accumulation that doesn't require positing a substantial
    doṣa-reservoir?
  · Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam sūtra 5 places citi itself in the position of universal
    cause-of-manifestation. This is structurally more sophisticated than Sāṃkhya's
    Prakṛti-as-cause but raises the same problem: a cause with intrinsic causal
    nature. How would Kṣemarāja respond to MMK 1's analysis of causation?

Five-part analysis — YTT Module 3 (Yoga Nidra):

  1. WHAT THE YTT TEACHES:
  The YTT presents four states of consciousness (waking/jāgrat, dream/svapna,
  deep sleep/suṣupti, enlightenment/turīya) as a yogic map of the mind's states.
  Yoga Nidra is positioned as a practice that cultivates the alpha-state (between
  waking and dreaming), over time leading practitioners to turīya — a state of
  complete harmony and "Pure Awareness" in which projections cease. The manual
  states: "Awareness is the boundless, formless, and timeless Ātman within us
  all. It has no beginning or end. It is indestructible. This awareness is the
  Self that we long to know." [The five-part analysis continues in the source
  document through its full development — the excerpt in the source file for
  Module D ends at this point in Week 4's What the YTT Teaches section, with the
  complete analysis for this module contained in the following sections.]

  KEY TENSIONS flagged for this module (from the Shaiva counterpoint and
  cross-curriculum notes):
  · Kṣemarāja's PH sūtra 18 frames turīya and turyātīta as states unified by
    Śiva-consciousness — the most sophisticated possible defense of a Self that
    pervades all four states.
  · The Buddhist anātman account: nirbīja samādhi (Patañjali's Sūtra 1.51)
    extinguishes even the seed of self-concept — the question of what remains

  · Dzogchen's YNS Weeks 4–5 provide the rang rig analysis — self-knowing
    awareness distinguished from the Trika's vimarśa: rang rig is not a Self
    knowing itself but awareness without a center recognizing its own nature.
  · Module C Week 3 (AKBh Ch. 1 on unconditioned dharmas and cessation) provides
    the Abhidharma counter to turīya as the "fourth state" — analytical cessation
    (pratisaṃkhyā-nirodha) is simply an absence, not a positive state of bliss-
    identity.
